Helping Home

Using Smart Home Technology to Improve the Lives of Caregivers

Summary

Our research found that 1 in 8 adults suffer from some form of memory loss and 5.8 million Americans are living with Alzheimer's disease. By 2050, that number is expected to rise to 14 million. We also found that the strain of being a caregiver increases the risk of illness and even death. Our team's project, Helping Home, was created to improve the lives of caregivers and family members of patients with Alzhemer's and other forms of memory loss.

The challenge

  • Understanding dementia and the different stages of Alzheimer's.
  • Discovering how caretakers may benefit from smart home technology.
  • Create a non-intrusive solution.

Our approach

  • Providing caretakers a way to track the daily activities of patients with dementia.
  • Developing an intuitive alerts system.
  • Implementing new technology to create a system of smart home devices.

Understanding Our Users

Brainstorming with my Teach Access team.

Our personas are of Nola, a 76-year-old patient with Alzheimer’s Disease, her partner Albert, and their daughter Marge. Albert and Marge care for Nola, but sacrifice time for themselves. This is a very common problem for families and loved ones of patients with memory loss. We wanted to improve this situation that many Americans face today.

Original Concept

The original concept worked directly inside the google home app. Our idea for adaptive alerts was to use Artificial Intelligence and machine learning to learn the behaviors and habits of patients.

Google Home menu with Helping Home.

My Day is a schedule of the patient’s day, created by the caretaker. The Habits section records and tracks the task completions. At any time, a caretaker or medical professional can view the progress and history of a patient’s day.

Taking a Step Back

After working on this project for a week during our Study Away trip in Silicon Valley, I reviewed the objectives and mission of the project. I realized that our original concepts had some major problem areas:

1. Creating reminders was too complicated.

2. User research found the UX was confusing.

3. Setting a firm schedule removes the freedom and independence of the patient with AD.

4. Removing the caretaker would reduce valuable time socializing.


I decided to redesign the user experience and redefine the mission of our product. It didn’t make sense to make someone adhere to a caretaker’s schedule. Instead, the application tracks the patient’s activity in certain key areas which gives the caretaker peace of mind. The application will also alert the caretaker of any unusual activity.

Logging Your Day

The application relies on a variety of sensors that are capable of tracking things like opening doors or cabinets, or sitting in or out of a chair. These sensors will allow a caretaker to monitor their patient's activity throughout the day. Creating a new alert is simple. All a caretaker has to do is select the sensor, the action, and the duration. For some alerts, like medicine, a caretaker can import the schedule from prerecorded medication.
